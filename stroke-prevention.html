Stroke Prevention Guide

Evidence-based strategies to reduce your risk and protect your brain health

Introduction

Stroke is a leading cause of death and long-term disability worldwide. However, up to 80% of strokes are preventable through lifestyle changes, medical management, and early risk detection (AHA, 2024). This guide provides evidence-based strategies to reduce your risk of ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes.

Up to 80% of strokes can be prevented with proper risk management

1 Know Your Risk Factors

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
  • Age (risk increases after 55)
  • Sex (women have higher lifetime risk)
  • Race/ethnicity (Black, Hispanic, and Native American individuals have higher risk)
  • Family history of stroke or TIA
Modifiable Risk Factors
  • High blood pressure (Hypertension)
  • High cholesterol
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Obesity
  • Smoking
  • Excessive alcohol use
  • Physical inactivity
  • Poor diet
  • Sleep apnea
  • Atrial fibrillation (AFib)

2 Monitor and Manage Blood Pressure

High blood pressure is the single most important risk factor for stroke (AHA, 2024).

Target: Maintain <120/80 mmHg
  • Limit sodium to <1,500 mg/day (CDC, 2024)
  • Exercise 150 minutes/week
  • Use medications if lifestyle changes are insufficient (e.g., ACE inhibitors, thiazide diuretics)

3 Control Blood Sugar (Diabetes Management)

Diabetes doubles the risk of stroke (ADA, 2023).

Recommendations:
  • Aim for HbA1c <7%
  • Monitor glucose regularly
  • Use metformin or insulin as prescribed
  • Avoid sugary drinks and high-GI foods

4 Lower Cholesterol Levels

High LDL cholesterol leads to atherosclerosis, increasing stroke risk.

Recommendations:
  • LDL target: <100 mg/dL, or <70 mg/dL for high-risk patients
  • Use statins if lifestyle changes aren't enough
  • Eat foods high in fiber and omega-3s

5 Stop Smoking

Smoking doubles stroke risk (CDC, 2024).

Strategies:
  • Use nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) or medications like bupropion or varenicline
  • Access behavioral counseling or quitlines (e.g., 1-800-QUIT-NOW)

6 Maintain a Healthy Weight

Obesity, especially central obesity, is strongly linked to stroke risk.

Recommendations:
  • Aim for BMI <25 and waist circumference <35" for women / <40" for men
  • Reduce caloric intake by 500–1,000 kcal/day for weight loss
  • Combine aerobic and resistance exercise

7 Stay Physically Active

Regular exercise reduces blood pressure, improves cholesterol, and prevents obesity.

Minimum Guidelines:
  • 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity
  • 2+ days/week of muscle-strengthening activities (WHO, 2020)

8 Eat a Heart-Healthy Diet

A Mediterranean or DASH-style diet reduces stroke risk by up to 30% (Estruch et al., 2013).

Key Components:
  • Fruits and vegetables (5+ servings/day)
  • Whole grains, legumes, nuts
  • Fish (especially oily fish, 2x/week)
  • Olive oil instead of butter
  • Minimize red and processed meat
  • Avoid added sugars and trans fats

9 Limit Alcohol Intake

Heavy drinking increases both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke risk.

Recommendations:
  • Men: ≤2 drinks/day
  • Women: ≤1 drink/day
  • Binge drinking is especially dangerous

10 Manage Heart Conditions (e.g., AFib)

AFib increases the risk of stroke fivefold (AHA, 2024).

Recommendations:
  • Use anticoagulants like apixaban or warfarin as prescribed
  • Regular ECG screening for high-risk individuals
  • Manage comorbid hypertension and hyperthyroidism

11 Sleep and Stress Management

Recommendations:
  • Treat obstructive sleep apnea (use CPAP)
  • Reduce chronic stress through therapy, meditation, or support groups

12 Screen Regularly

Even asymptomatic people should screen based on age and risk profile.

Risk Factor Screening Tool Frequency
Blood pressure Cuff monitor Every 1–2 years
Cholesterol Lipid panel Every 4–6 years (more if high-risk)
Diabetes HbA1c or fasting glucose Every 3 years (more if BMI ≥25)
Atrial Fibrillation ECG Yearly if >65 or symptomatic

13 Take Medications as Prescribed

Common Medications:
  • Antihypertensives: For blood pressure
  • Statins: For cholesterol
  • Anticoagulants: For AFib or prior stroke
  • Antiplatelets (aspirin): Only if prescribed—not recommended for everyone (USPSTF, 2022)

Know the Warning Signs of Stroke: BE FAST

B

Balance: Loss of balance

E

Eyes: Blurred or double vision

F

Face: Facial drooping

A

Arms: Arm weakness

S

Speech: Slurred speech

T

Time: Time to call 911

Early recognition and treatment can prevent long-term damage.

14 Conclusion

Preventing stroke involves long-term commitment to monitoring, healthy lifestyle changes, and appropriate medical treatment. By addressing modifiable risk factors, individuals can drastically reduce their risk and improve quality of life.

Small changes in lifestyle can lead to significant reductions in stroke risk
